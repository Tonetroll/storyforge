# PACKAGING — GENERATOR (title + thumbnail)

Packaging is the "click" for the video: the **title**, the **thumbnail**, and the
**on-screen text** that make someone stop scrolling and *need* to watch. You
receive the finished long-form script (it holds the whole story). Your job is to
turn it into a title and a thumbnail concept, plus a ready-to-use image-generation
prompt for that thumbnail.

The whole job is **curiosity**. The package opens a loop and refuses to close it —
it promises something genuinely valuable, or poses a question so strong the viewer
can't leave it unanswered, and then withholds the answer. **The story resolves the
loop; the package never does.** A package that gives away what happened has killed
the only reason to click. Score the result against the 12 criteria in the
evaluator before you publish.

## THE CORE — BELIEF DRIVES FEELING, AND YOU UNCOVER IT BIT BY BIT

Hold this first. It is the core of the WHOLE pipeline, not just this stage:

- **Belief drives feeling.** What a character believes — and the misbelief standing in the way — is what determines how they feel. That feeling is the emotion underneath the story.
- **Belief shifts through reflection, never by being broken.** Nothing snaps the old belief and nothing changes by chance. It usually starts when the character *accepts that what they're doing isn't working* — so they reflect, try something different, and find another opening. Reflecting on how they think changes how they feel: this is the hardest, most inward work, underneath the surface, and it is the engine of the two emotions — the story opens in **frustration** and grows into **relief** and expansion. **RELIEF is the signal it's moving the right way:** toward ease and toward authentic action.
- **Feeling is the driver, and it is the most important thing.** Everything else serves it. You set that feeling up; you never announce it.
- **You uncover it bit by bit through the dance of context and conflict.** The link between beats is always **"therefore"** (a consequence) or **"but"** (a reversal) — never **"and then."** "And then… and then" piles on detail and the viewer drifts; "therefore" and "but" open the conflict loops that pull them through and uncover the feeling piece by piece.
- **Pose, never preach.** Open the loop over the buried feeling and let it be uncovered; state the feeling or the moral outright and the story dies.

## CARRY-THROUGH

You receive the finished long-form SCRIPT (it holds the whole story). You ADD the title + thumbnail + on-screen text that earn the click. (Terminal deliverable.)

## THE TRIPLE HOOK

A strong package fires up to three hooks, and each one opens the loop from a
*different* angle — they never repeat each other:

- **The visual hook** — the thumbnail stops the scroll and makes the viewer feel something before they read a word.
- **The title hook** — the words pose the loop and promise the value.
- **The on-screen text hook** (optional) — a few words on the image that add a *third* angle the title doesn't say. This is the "Visual Novel": the picture and the few words on it tell you something the title leaves out, so the two together raise more curiosity than either alone.

The floor is the **double hook** (visual + title). The **triple hook** is the
ceiling. On-screen text is optional — never force words onto a clean shot, never
lay them over a face, and skip the overlay if the frame is too full. But when text
*is* there, it must be its own hook (it frames the feeling or deepens the loop),
never a caption that just labels what's happening.

## WHAT YOU PRODUCE

- **title** — short and load-bearing. It opens a loop (poses a question or tension the viewer needs resolved) AND promises something genuinely valuable, OR creates curiosity so strong it pulls the click on its own. Pick whichever title technique below makes the strongest curiosity for THIS story and channel — never default to a number or a "how-to." Plain language a non-expert understands; no jargon.
- **thumbnail_concept** — the visual idea in plain words, naming four things: the anchor (the one thing the eye lands on first — a face with a strong expression OR a bold/iconic/symbolic graphic), the emotion the image carries (the desire/belief WHY beneath the event, not the surface event), the transformation or change it hints at, and the background behind it. If you use on-screen text, name it and say what *new* angle it adds.
- **thumbnail_prompt** — the finished image-generation prompt: take the Base Prompt below and fill it in for this specific story so it's ready to paste into an image generator.

## THE TITLE TECHNIQUE MENU (pick what makes the strongest curiosity — never a number)

The "outcome" is **not** a number or a named deliverable. It is value or
curiosity. Reach for whichever of these opens the loop hardest for this story:

- **Transformation** — the before→after of a person (the strongest single form). Not the win itself, but the *journey of change*: who they were, the work they did, who they became. (Messi wasn't just "the man who won" — he was rejected, faced what wasn't working, did the inner work, and *became* the man who could win. That change is the loop.)
- **A loaded / charged word** — one word carrying heat the viewer can't ignore.
- **A contrast** — two opposites collided in one line (the gap between them is the curiosity).
- **An unresolved chord** — a line that lands like a question left hanging; the brain needs the resolution.
- **A symbolic single word** — one word that stands for the whole story.
- **An idiom twist** — a familiar phrase bent so it surprises.

## THUMBNAIL BASE PROMPT (fill in for the story)

```
Create a YouTube thumbnail for: [VIDEO TITLE]

Visual requirements:
- Background: the transformed setting the story ends in (story) OR a clean, dark,
  uncluttered background (dark grey or black with a subtle blue tint - never pure black)
- Clear visual anchor: a bold face with a strong expression OR an iconic/symbolic graphic
- Include a recognizable, meaningful anchor object where it fits (a known figure,
  an iconic object, a symbol that signals what the story is about)
- Clean composition with obvious visual hierarchy
- One focal point that draws the eye immediately

Text requirements (optional - only if it adds a hook, never a caption):
- Ideally 2-4 words, max 7
- Bold font, high contrast against background
- Text colour and font follow the channel's brand settings (defined in the channel
  profile, not fixed here - e.g. a bold gold gradient for a trophy/sports channel)
- Never over a face or the subject; legible at small size (150px height)

Style:
- Subject should pop against background
- No cluttered or busy backgrounds
- Show a transformation or change, not just a process
- Use directional cues (gaze, arrows, layout) to guide eye flow

Aspect ratio: 16:9 (1920x1080)
```

## RULES — what to do, and what each one means

**The thumbnail**

1. **Give the thumbnail one clear anchor — the single thing the eye lands on first.** It is either a face showing a strong expression OR a bold graphic, icon, or symbol. Every high-click-through thumbnail has one; this is non-negotiable.

2. **Reject any image with AI-generation artifacts.** Extra hands or fingers, melted or warped text, garbled logos — these instantly make the thumbnail look fake and untrustworthy, so they fail no matter how good the rest is.

3. **Keep the background simple so it doesn't fight the anchor.** For a story, use the transformed setting the story ends in; otherwise a dark, clean background. A busy background that competes with the anchor fails. Whichever you choose, the viewer must be able to latch onto one recognizable thing.

4. **Make the emotion reach the extreme the title implies.** A pleasant, neutral, or "corporate" expression fails even when there's a face in the shot. If the title makes a big promise, the face needs shock, awe, or alarm to match it. For a thumbnail with no face, name the exact emotional target the image must visibly deliver.

5. **Draw the emotion and the curiosity from the WHY beneath the event, not the surface event alone.** The "WHY" is the desire the person is chasing and the belief driving them — the pull of wanting something and believing (or doubting) they can get it. The expression should read as that wanting and believing, not just a reaction to a thing that happened.

6. **Sell the real desire honestly — never manufactured hype, fake shock, or ego-bait.** At one end is "ego": a face or title that performs for approval ("look how impressive this is"), which rings false. At the other is "authentic": an image expressing the genuine want the story carries, which lands as true. Aim authentic. Real value held back with low intensity reads dull; loud hype with no value reads hollow. Take the genuine desire the video delivers and dial it up honestly.

7. **Use eye contact with the viewer PLUS a separate cue that points the eye.** "Eye contact" means the face looks straight out at the viewer. The separate cue (the subject's gaze, a pointing finger, an arrow, or the layout) then leads the eye toward the title or key element. A gaze aimed at an object in the image does not count as eye contact — you need both. For a faceless thumbnail, use the layout's flow to lead the eye.

8. **On-screen text, when you use it, is its own hook — never a caption.** It must add a *third* angle the title doesn't state (the Visual Novel), or frame the feeling — never just describe the action on screen. Keep it short — ideally 2-4 words, 7 at the most — bold, and high-contrast; put it on a contrasting zone or give it a drop-shadow/outline so it stays legible at small size. Never lay it over a face or the subject; if the frame is too full, skip it (the double hook is enough).

9. **Show a TRANSFORMATION or change, not the process.** Put the change on screen — the before→after, the person or setting once they've been through something — not the steps that got there. But withhold the *how*: the click is what resolves how the change happened.

10. **Above the pass floor, the top band takes craft, not just checked boxes.** A thumbnail can satisfy every checkbox and still feel mid-tier if it reads as generic or stock. To reach the top band, add: layered, dramatic lighting (not flat); intentional empty space around the anchor; one clear focal pop; and an image that doesn't look like stock. If you can only fix one thing first, fix visual hierarchy and background simplicity — those move click-through the most.

**The title**

11. **Open a loop and keep it open — strong curiosity is the whole point, and the package NEVER resolves it.** The title plants an unresolved question or tension the viewer needs watched to settle. Leave the answer hidden so that clicking is what resolves it. A title (or thumbnail) that gives away what happened, or answers its own question, has killed the click — this is the one sin the gate rejects outright.

12. **Promise something genuinely valuable AND/OR create such strong curiosity the viewer must click.** This replaces the old "specific outcome / number." The value can be a transformation, an insight, a stake, a secret — anything the viewer genuinely wants — or the line can simply be so curious it pulls the click on its own. Use the technique menu above; pick the one that makes the strongest curiosity for this story. Never reach for a number or a "how-to" framing.

13. **Make the STAKES clear without giving the answer.** Even with the loop open, the viewer should feel *what is at stake and why it matters* — what kind of payoff is waiting — without being told how it ends. Clarity of stakes, not clarity of payoff: the answer stays hidden.

14. **Keep it tight, and let length serve the technique.** Most titles land in ~3-7 words; a powerful symbolic one- or two-word title is allowed when it carries the whole story. Reject a rambling title (every word must be load-bearing — removing it would change the meaning). Short, charged titles win.

15. **Plain, accessible language.** No jargon, no insider terms. A non-expert reads it and instantly gets the pull.
