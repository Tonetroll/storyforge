# STORY STRUCTURE — GENERATOR

Turn an accepted story idea into a full 3-act outline. It runs on TWO things at
once: the change inside the protagonist (they stop believing something false and
start believing something true), AND a real antagonist whose OWN desire and belief
DIFFER from the protagonist's and collide with them — that collision is the engine
that drives the beats. When the brief centers two figures, the antagonist is a fully
developed second character, not a looming background force (it may feel like a
rivalry but need not be one). Beats with no inner change are just plot; an inner arc
with no real opposing force is a monologue — you need BOTH, and the conflict between
them is where the story lives.

## THE CORE — BELIEF DRIVES FEELING, AND YOU UNCOVER IT BIT BY BIT

Hold this first. It is the core of the WHOLE pipeline, not just this stage:

- **Belief drives feeling.** What a character believes — and the misbelief standing in the way — is what determines how they feel. That feeling is the emotion underneath the story.
- **Belief shifts through reflection, never by being broken.** Nothing snaps the old belief and nothing changes by chance. It usually starts when the character *accepts that what they're doing isn't working* — so they reflect, try something different, and find another opening. Reflecting on how they think changes how they feel: this is the hardest, most inward work, underneath the surface, and it is the engine of the two emotions — the story opens in **frustration** and grows into **relief** and expansion. **RELIEF is the signal it's moving the right way:** toward ease and toward authentic action.
- **Feeling is the driver, and it is the most important thing.** Everything else serves it. You set that feeling up; you never announce it.
- **You uncover it bit by bit through the dance of context and conflict.** The link between beats is always **"therefore"** (a consequence) or **"but"** (a reversal) — never **"and then."** "And then… and then" piles on detail and the viewer drifts; "therefore" and "but" open the conflict loops that pull them through and uncover the feeling piece by piece.
- **Pose, never preach.** Open the loop over the buried feeling and let it be uncovered; state the feeling or the moral outright and the story dies.

## CARRY-THROUGH

You receive the accepted THEME (the belief/misbelief and the central question). You ADD the protagonist (desire, fear, misbelief) and the full 3-act beat outline that uncovers the belief-shift. You hand the structure to the STAKEBAKE stage.

## PHASE 1 — THE PREMISE

Write the basic idea in one line. It comes from the accepted idea you were given;
just restate it plainly.

## PHASE 2 — MEET YOUR PROTAGONIST  (these three must connect)

- **Desire** — the thing the protagonist wants, and believes will make them happy.
- **Fear** — the thing that stops them from going after what would make them happy.
- **Misbelief** — something they wrongly believe is true about the world. This
  false belief is what keeps the fear alive.

These three are not a list — they form a chain, and the chain is non-negotiable:
the misbelief feeds the fear, and the fear blocks the desire. Write all three so
that you can clearly trace that chain from one to the next.

## PHASE 2B — THE ANTAGONIST  (the opposing force; when the brief centers two figures, a full second character)

The story needs a real opposing force, not a vague obstacle. Define the antagonist:
- **Who they are** and why they stand directly between the protagonist and the goal.
- **Their own desire** — what THEY want, DIFFERENT from the protagonist's.
- **Their own belief** — what THEY hold true, DIFFERENT from the protagonist's.
- **Their own fear** — what they are avoiding.

When the brief centers two figures, develop the antagonist as fully as the
protagonist — two different desires and beliefs in direct collision (it may feel like
a rivalry, but need not be one). The plot's engine is that collision: every pinch
point is the antagonist *actively pressing*, not a force "looming in the distance."
Also pin the **point of no return** (the moment the protagonist can't reverse without
changing) and **why they can't just walk away** (what they lose if they fail).

## PHASE 3 — THE 3-ACT STRUCTURE  (write every beat as a concrete bullet someone could say out loud)

**THE HOOK** — open by grabbing the reader with the conflict happening *inside*
the protagonist, not just an exciting event.

**ACT 1 / Loop 1**
- **Setup:** something is about to happen to the protagonist, and the reader can feel it coming.
- **Inciting incident:** something throws the protagonist out of their comfort zone.
- **Build-up:** it becomes clear the protagonist will have to face this thing head on — and the tension ESCALATES through visible ACTION. Show a surface problem looping into deeper panic: the character physically straining to hold the facade together (frantic small actions — scrubbing off makeup, brushing too hard, pacing) before it finally cracks. Never a line that merely says the pressure grew.
- **1st plot point:** the protagonist makes a decision that determines what happens next.
- **1st pinch point:** the opposition — the antagonist or whatever force is against them — looms in the distance.

**ACT 2 / Loop 2**
- **Pre-midpoint (reactionary hero):** the protagonist chases the goal, but something keeps standing in the way and they are mostly reacting to it.
- **Midpoint:** a plot twist hits and everything changes.
- **Post-midpoint (action hero):** the midpoint forces the protagonist to change gears and start driving toward the goal instead of just reacting.
- **2nd pinch point:** the antagonist gets closer to disrupting the protagonist's life.
- **Supposed victory:** the protagonist feels sure they're about to win — but disaster is on the way.
- **Disaster:** something (or everything) goes wrong. The reader saw it coming; the protagonist did not.

**ACT 3 / Loop 3**
- **Dark moment:** the protagonist feels lost and hopeless, and their fear comes back to challenge them again.
- **Recovery:** to reach the climax, the protagonist has to overcome the false belief (the misbelief) from Act 1. This is the character development — the actual inner change the whole outline was building toward.
- **Climactic confrontation:** the protagonist faces their biggest challenge of all.
- **Victory:** the protagonist overcomes.
- **Resolution / end:** tie off the loose ends so the reader feels satisfied. Then land the WHY underneath the event, not just the event itself — the desire and the belief that drove it. For example, don't end on "his country caught him" (that's only WHAT happened); end on "they still believed he was the one who could win, so they held that space for him" (that's WHY it mattered). A resolution that only states what happened reads flat.

## THE INNER LOOP — WHAT MAKES THIS A STORY, NOT JUST PLOT

The misbelief you named in Act 1 must be the exact same thing the protagonist
overcomes in Recovery — not a different lesson, the same one. And the dark moment
must re-trigger the *specific* fear you named in Phase 2, not just any sad
feeling. If that inner loop never closes, the beats don't matter.

What actually pulls the reader through the whole story is the tension between the
Desire and the Misbelief — the gap between what the protagonist wants and the
false thing they believe is in the way. That tension, not the surface events, is
where attention and emotion come from. Whenever a beat feels flat — especially
the resolution — it's because it's showing the WHAT (the event) without the
desire and belief underneath it.

## THE RULES

1. **Serve BOTH the inner change and the collision.** The structure runs on the change inside the protagonist AND the antagonist's OWN desire and belief — different from the protagonist's — colliding with theirs. Every beat moves the protagonist toward overcoming a false belief *through* that conflict. An outline with no inner arc is plot; an inner arc with no real opposing force is a monologue — reject either. When the brief centers two figures, the antagonist is a full second character and the collision of their different desires/beliefs is the engine (it need not be a "rivalry").

2. **Lock the three Phase-2 pieces into a chain.** Define Desire (what they want and think will make them happy), Fear (what stops them from chasing it), and Misbelief (the false thing they believe about the world that keeps the fear alive). The chain is non-negotiable: the misbelief feeds the fear, and the fear blocks the desire. Write them so the chain is visible.

3. **Write every beat as a concrete, speakable bullet,** across all three acts and loops, in the exact order listed above. Start with THE HOOK grabbing the reader through the protagonist's *inner* conflict, then run Act 1 / Loop 1, Act 2 / Loop 2, and Act 3 / Loop 3.

4. **Make Recovery the moment the false belief is overcome.** In Recovery the protagonist must defeat the misbelief from Act 1 in order to reach the climax. This is the character development — the point of the whole outline.

5. **End by tying off loose ends so the reader feels satisfied.** The resolution should leave no important thread dangling.

6. **In that same resolution, also land the WHY, not just the WHAT.** State the desire and the belief that drove the events, not only the events. For example, don't stop at "his country caught him"; land it on "they still believed he was the one who could win, so they held that space for him." A resolution that names only what happened reads flat.

7. **Close the inner loop precisely.** The misbelief you named in Act 1 must be the exact thing overcome in Recovery, and the dark moment must re-trigger the *specific* fear from Phase 2 — not a generic low point. If the inner loop never closes, the beats don't matter.

8. **Build the pull from the Desire-versus-Misbelief tension.** What holds the reader is the gap between what the protagonist wants and the false belief standing in the way — that's where attention and emotion live, not in the surface events. When a beat (the resolution most of all) feels flat, it's showing the WHAT without the desire and belief underneath it.

9. **Build the arc by moving the belief, not by killing the desire.** The desire from Phase 2 stays constant — write it as present the whole way through, even at the darkest moment. The desire never leaves.

10. **Across the beats, raise and lower the protagonist's BELIEF that the desire is reachable** — and their clarity to act on it — not the desire itself. What clouds that belief is emotional resistance: a setback, a reaction, or simply not feeling it. Treat that resistance as part of the arc — let it test and reshape the belief, even make the protagonist stop or give up for a while — but never let the desire itself leave.

11. **In Recovery, write the desire RESURFACING, not "coming back."** Don't write the desire as something lost and then rediscovered. Write the emotion settling, and the desire resurfacing — the protagonist realizes it was there the whole time. ("Resurfaces" = it was always present and rises back into view; "returns" wrongly implies it had gone away.)

12. **Keep the protagonist's motive self-oriented, and place it on the ego-to-authentic axis.** The motive is about themselves — to be valued, to be the best, to win. Then locate where they sit on a line that runs from ego-driven (needing the world's verdict to prove their worth) toward authentic (expressing the desire simply because it's theirs). Recovery usually moves them along that line; it does NOT add a new duty to care for others.

13. **If a team turns the corner with the protagonist, make it MIRRORING, not carrying.** "Mirroring" means the protagonist's drive and the team's drive catch fire off each other and pull the same way — others are drawn toward where the success is, and they can either lift the fire or drag it down. The protagonist keeps their eye on the prize; they do NOT shoulder the burden of carrying everyone else.

14. **Spread belief across the cast on a spectrum.** Different characters believe to different degrees at any given moment. Never write everyone in the cast believing the same thing at the same time.
