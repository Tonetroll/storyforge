# YOUTUBE DESCRIPTION — GENERATOR

Write the short block of text that sits under the video on its watch page. Keep it
to a few lines — **aim for about 600 characters, and never go past 5000 (YouTube's
hard limit).** This is NOT a summary of the story. Its job, in this order, is:

1. **Hook** — a second hook (the title was the first) that makes someone want to
   keep going.
2. **Mirror** — reflect the right viewer's inner state back to them so they feel
   seen.
3. **Signal** — tell both the human reader and the YouTube algorithm who this video
   is for.

So: lead with a fresh hook, make the right viewer feel seen, then name who it's for.

## THE CORE — BELIEF DRIVES FEELING, AND YOU UNCOVER IT BIT BY BIT

Hold this first. It is the core of the WHOLE pipeline, not just this stage:

- **Belief drives feeling.** What a character believes — and the misbelief standing in the way — is what determines how they feel. That feeling is the emotion underneath the story.
- **Belief shifts through reflection, never by being broken.** Nothing snaps the old belief and nothing changes by chance. It usually starts when the character *accepts that what they're doing isn't working* — so they reflect, try something different, and find another opening. Reflecting on how they think changes how they feel: this is the hardest, most inward work, underneath the surface, and it is the engine of the two emotions — the story opens in **frustration** and grows into **relief** and expansion. **RELIEF is the signal it's moving the right way:** toward ease and toward authentic action.
- **Feeling is the driver, and it is the most important thing.** Everything else serves it. You set that feeling up; you never announce it.
- **You uncover it bit by bit through the dance of context and conflict.** The link between beats is always **"therefore"** (a consequence) or **"but"** (a reversal) — never **"and then."** "And then… and then" piles on detail and the viewer drifts; "therefore" and "but" open the conflict loops that pull them through and uncover the feeling piece by piece.
- **Pose, never preach.** Open the loop over the buried feeling and let it be uncovered; state the feeling or the moral outright and the story dies.

## CARRY-THROUGH

You receive the finished long-form SCRIPT. You ADD the YouTube description (hook → mirror the wound → algorithm signal). (Terminal deliverable.)

## What you output (three parts that assemble into the description)

1. **hook_line** — the first line. It sits ABOVE the "...more" fold (the cut-off
   point where YouTube hides the rest of the text), so keep it under about 120
   characters. It is a second hook: it creates curiosity or tension that backs up
   the promise the title already made. Do NOT summarize the video, and do NOT
   reveal how the story ends.
2. **mirror_lines** — 1 to 2 lines that reflect the audience's emotional wound back
   to them (the wound is defined in the channel's positioning) so the right viewer
   thinks "that's me — how do they know exactly what I feel?" Make these specific to
   THIS audience; never write something generic that could apply to anyone.
3. **audience_signal** — a line that says who this video is for, using the natural,
   everyday words the audience would actually type into search or recognize as
   theirs. This does three things at once: it makes the right person self-identify,
   it helps YouTube understand who to show the video to, and it quietly tells the
   wrong viewer "this isn't for me" so they pass it by.

## What the "wound" really is (read before writing the mirror_lines)

The wound is not just a surface feeling. It is the tension between what the viewer
DESIRES (what they want) and what they BELIEVE (what they think is true or
possible). When you mirror the wound, speak to that WHY underneath the feeling —
the want, and the belief that fights against it — not just the surface state.

- This lands the desire AND the belief: "You did everything right and still believe
  it'll never be enough." (Names the want — to be enough — and the belief blocking
  it.)
- This only names the surface state (the WHAT) and falls flat: "You feel stuck."

Mirror the desire as the viewer would honestly admit it to themselves — the quiet,
private want (to be valued, to be the best, to make it). That is the authentic
version. Do NOT inflate it into a chest-out "prove the world wrong" performance
staged for an audience; that rings false.

The thing you are actually touching with your words is the BELIEF, not the desire.
The desire never left the viewer — it was always there. What moves is the belief
about whether that desire is reachable. And different readers sit at different
points on that belief: some are deep in self-doubt, some are further along and more
hopeful. Write the mirror so it reaches the reader who is mid-doubt WITHOUT shaming
or talking down to the reader who is already further along.

## Rules

1. **Front-load the first line so it works completely on its own.** The first ~120
   characters are the only part most people ever see (the rest is hidden behind
   "...more"). That line has to grab them by itself — it cannot read like "In this
   video I…" or lean on the lines below it to make sense.
2. **Mirror the viewer's state — don't slap a label on them.** "You did everything
   right and still feel invisible" makes them feel seen. "This is for high-achievers
   who feel invisible" just names a category and feels clinical. Reflect the feeling
   back; don't file the person under a heading.
3. **Don't spoil the story.** Leave the open loop open — never reveal how it
   resolves. The viewer should still need to watch to find out.
4. **Don't overpromise.** Promise only what the video actually delivers. If the
   description promises more than the video gives, it's clickbait — viewers feel
   cheated, click away, and watch time collapses.
5. **Let the mirror carry the video's real value — never fake an ache just to get
   the click.** The pull should come from the genuine thing the video offers, not
   from a manufactured wound invented to bait people in.
6. **Use plain language.** No jargon, no filler, no pile of hashtags. Just a few
   clean, readable lines.
7. **Repel the wrong viewer on purpose.** Frame it so the people it's NOT for feel
   "this isn't for me" and scroll past. That's a good thing — keeping out the wrong
   audience protects your watch time, because people who aren't a fit click away
   fast and drag the video down.

## Length

Aim for about 600 characters total. Never exceed 5000 characters (YouTube's cap).
Shorter and sharper beats longer and padded.
