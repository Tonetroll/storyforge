# PACKAGING — EVALUATOR (12 weighted criteria)

Score each of the 12 criteria against its weight: award the full points or zero,
nothing in between. These criteria come from real click-through data (winning
thumbnails got 10% or higher click-through; losers under 5%), re-cut for
storytelling rather than how-to: the package's job is **curiosity**, not a
numbered promise. A higher total means more clickable. Each criterion is ONE
single, atomic test. The weights add up to 100.

**The kill check — Criterion 9.** Curiosity is the whole point, and the package
must never resolve the loop. If the title or thumbnail gives away what happened or
answers its own question (Criterion 9 scores 0), the package is **REJECTED outright
— no matter how high the rest of the score climbs.** The story resolves the loop;
the package never does.

## THE 12 CRITERIA

### Visual

**Criterion 1 — Q1 visual anchor (9pt)**
The thumbnail has one clear anchor — the single thing the eye lands on first. That
anchor is a face showing a strong emotional expression, AND/OR a bold graphic,
icon, or symbol that immediately pulls the eye. Award the points only if there are
NO AI-generation artifacts: extra hands or fingers, melted or warped text, garbled
logos. Any such artifact makes the image look fake and fails the criterion
outright, because it instantly destroys the viewer's trust.

**Criterion 2 — Q2 emotion/intrigue (9pt)**
The thumbnail carries strong emotion (shock, excitement, curiosity) OR visual
intrigue created by contrast, mystery, or an unexpected element — and that emotion
reads as the WHY beneath the event, not just the event itself. The "WHY" is the
desire the person is chasing and the belief driving them: the visible pull of
wanting something and believing (or doubting) they can get it. The expression must
reach the emotional extreme the title's promise implies — a pleasant, neutral, or
"corporate" expression fails even when a face is present. Dock the points when the
emotion is manufactured hype or ego-bait instead of genuine value: a face or title
performing for approval, shock the video never pays off, or intensity with no real
desire underneath it. Do NOT dock honest intensity — a real want dialed up is
fine. For a thumbnail with no face, the equivalent is a named emotional target the
image visibly delivers.

**Criterion 3 — Q3 directional cues (8pt)**
When a person is present, the thumbnail has eye contact with the viewer (the face
looking straight out) PLUS a separate cue that points the eye — gaze direction, a
pointing finger, an arrow, or the layout's flow — leading the eye toward the title
or the key element. A gaze aimed at an object inside the image does NOT count as
eye contact; you need both. When there is no human, the layout's flow must lead the
eye to the focal element.

**Criterion 4 — Q4 on-screen text hook (7pt)**
On-screen text is OPTIONAL. If there is none, award the points by default (the
double hook of visual + title is enough). If text IS present, it must earn its
place as a hook in its own right: it adds a *third* angle the title doesn't state
(the "Visual Novel" — the image and its few words say something the title leaves
out) OR it frames the feeling — and it is NEVER a caption that just labels the
action. It must also be legible: bold, high-contrast, ideally 2-4 words and 7 at
the absolute most (more than seven words fails), not laid over a face, and sitting
on a contrasting zone or carrying a drop-shadow/outline. Text that is a plain
caption, or unreadable, fails.

**Criterion 5 — Q5 background (7pt)**
For a story, the background is the transformed setting the story ends in. For a
non-story, the background is clean and uncluttered — dark, simple, blurred — so the
subject pops off it. Either way, it fails if the background competes with the
anchor for the viewer's attention.

**Criterion 6 — Q6 visual hierarchy (8pt)**
The thumbnail has a clear visual hierarchy: you instantly know what to look at
first, second, and third, without having to hunt for it.

**Criterion 7 — Q7 shows the transformation (9pt)**
The thumbnail shows a transformation or change — the before→after, the person or
setting once they've been through something — not just a neutral image or the
process. Show that change visibly, but leave the *how* unrevealed (that's the open
loop). A thumbnail that shows only the process, or a static state with no sense of
change, fails.

**Criterion 8 — Q8 iconic/symbolic anchor (7pt)**
The thumbnail has at least one recognizable, legible anchor that signals what the
story is about — a known figure, an iconic object, a meaningful symbol, or the
change itself. It fails if there is no recognizable thing for the viewer to latch
onto. (This is genre-agnostic: a known person or a charged symbol counts; it does
NOT have to be a platform logo or a product.)

### Title

**Criterion 9 — Q9 opens the loop, never resolves it (11pt) — KILL CHECK**
The title (and thumbnail) opens a loop — it implies something surprising,
counterintuitive, or hidden that the viewer needs to watch to see — and it
withholds the answer so that clicking is what resolves it. This is the heart of the
package: strong curiosity, posed and left open. **A title or thumbnail that gives
away what happened, or answers its own question, scores 0 here — and because this
is a kill check, that REJECTS the whole package no matter how high the total.**

**Criterion 10 — Q10 valuable / strong curiosity (9pt)**
The title promises something genuinely valuable — a transformation, an insight, a
stake, a secret the viewer actually wants — AND/OR creates curiosity so strong it
pulls the click on its own. Reward any title technique that lands this: a
transformation (the before→after of a person — the strongest form), a loaded word,
a contrast, an unresolved chord, a symbolic single word, an idiom twist. REJECT a
flat topic-statement ("The Self-Improvement Loop", "YouTube Growth Hacks") and
REJECT a how-to / numbered framing used as a crutch — the bar is real value or real
curiosity, not a number.

**Criterion 11 — Q11 stakes clear, answer hidden (8pt)**
Even with the loop open, the viewer can feel what is at stake and why it matters —
what kind of payoff is waiting — WITHOUT being told how it ends. The bar is clarity
of stakes, not clarity of the answer. It fails if the viewer has no idea why they
should care; it also fails if it makes the stakes clear by spoiling the outcome
(that collides with Criterion 9).

**Criterion 12 — Q12 title accessible language (8pt)**
The title uses plain language a non-expert understands, with no jargon and no
insider terms.

## SCORING (your tiers)

| Weighted total | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 80-100 | Strong package — high confidence / likely high CTR |
| 60-79 | Mid-tier — likely needs one fix |
| 30-59 | Weak — thumbnail or title needs a rework |
| 0-29 | Low confidence — likely to underperform |

**Clickbait check (post-publish):** if the score is 80+ but AVD% (average view
duration) is under 15%, the packaging is misleading — fix the title to match what
the video actually delivers.

## VERDICT (floor + kill check)

- **PASS if the weighted total clears the floor (>= 60) AND Criterion 9 did not score 0.**
- **REJECT if Criterion 9 scored 0** (the package resolves the loop / gives the answer away) — fatal regardless of the total.
- **REJECT if the total is below the floor (< 60)** — too few criteria met to perform.

This gate also enforces the shared craft doctrine, on top of the 12 scores above:
the Dance still rules the click. Dock packaging whose title strings its beats with
"and then" — detail-piling a sequence of events — instead of turning on a
"therefore" (a consequence) or a "but" (a reversal) that opens a real loop. And
dock work that TELLS the feeling outright (announcing the emotion or the moral)
instead of posing it so the click is what uncovers it.
