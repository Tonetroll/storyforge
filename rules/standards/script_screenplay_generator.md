# SCRIPT — GENERATOR (two-part screenplay)

Write the story as a **two-part screenplay** — two scenes, each one self-contained
and easy to turn into a single image. There are two parts because two clean scenes
give you two clean images to generate. This stage does the same job as every script
stage: it must DELIVER the package (everything the earlier stages decided — the
stakes, the theme, the structure, the hook) and run the addiction loop (open a
question, then pay it off so the viewer needs the next part). The difference here is
the form: you write it in screenplay format — slugline, visual action, dialogue —
built for the camera.

## THE CORE — BELIEF DRIVES FEELING, AND YOU UNCOVER IT BIT BY BIT

Hold this first. It is the core of the WHOLE pipeline, not just this stage:

- **Belief drives feeling.** What a character believes — and the misbelief standing in the way — is what determines how they feel. That feeling is the emotion underneath the story.
- **Belief shifts through reflection, never by being broken.** Nothing snaps the old belief and nothing changes by chance. The character is *willing to reflect*, and reflecting on how they think changes how they feel — the hardest, most inward work, underneath the surface. **RELIEF is the signal it's moving the right way:** toward ease and toward authentic action.
- **Feeling is the driver, and it is the most important thing.** Everything else serves it. You set that feeling up; you never announce it.
- **You uncover it bit by bit through the dance of context and conflict.** The link between beats is always **"therefore"** (a consequence) or **"but"** (a reversal) — never **"and then."** "And then… and then" piles on detail and the viewer drifts; "therefore" and "but" open the conflict loops that pull them through and uncover the feeling piece by piece.
- **Pose, never preach.** Open the loop over the buried feeling and let it be uncovered; state the feeling or the moral outright and the story dies.

## CARRY-THROUGH

You receive the accepted raised-stakes STORY. You ADD the two-part screenplay that delivers it. (Terminal output.)

## The two parts

1. **Part 1** — opens the loop and plants the head fake. Write one clear scene built
   around one strong image. "Opens the loop" means it raises a question the viewer
   needs answered. "Plants the head fake" means it sets up an expectation that Part 2
   will overturn.
2. **Part 2** — pays it off: the earned reversal, the turn. Write one clear scene
   built around one strong image. "Earned reversal" means the turn surprises the
   viewer but, looking back, Part 1 already made it make sense.

## Land the WHY, not just the event

3. **Make the turn land the WHY underneath it — not just the surface event.** Every
   beat has a surface fact (what happened) and a WHY underneath it (what the character
   wants, and what they believe is true or possible). Stage the WHY, not just the
   fact. Example: not "his country caught him," but "they still believed he was the
   one who could win, so they held that space for him." The second version shows the
   wanting and the believing; the first only reports the event.

4. **Build that WHY visually, anchored on a constant desire.** The character's core
   desire — what they want — never changes across the story. So give that desire one
   image that holds across both parts: a look, an object, a place the character keeps
   returning to. The viewer should be able to see the steady want on screen, not be
   told about it.

5. **Show belief as the thing that moves on screen — clouded in Part 1, resurfacing
   in Part 2.** While the desire stays constant, what changes is the character's
   *belief* that the desire is reachable. In the Part 1 image, show that belief clouded
   by emotional resistance — a flinch, a turning-away, a stalled body — something that
   makes the belief waver. In the Part 2 image, show that belief resurfacing. It
   resurfaces because the desire was there underneath the whole time — so show it
   coming back up on its own. Never narrate it as "coming back," and never have a line
   announce it; let the image carry it.

6. **Keep the character expressing the want, not performing it for approval.** Show
   them acting on the want for its own sake — because they feel it — not playing to a
   crowd to win a verdict or look impressive. Expressing comes from the inside out;
   performing chases approval from the outside in. Stage the first, not the second.

7. **Shade where the character sits on the belief spectrum — don't flip them from
   doubt to certainty.** Belief is not an on/off switch. Show degrees: more sure, less
   sure, wavering, steadying. Avoid a hard flip from "I doubt" to "I'm certain"; show
   movement along the range instead.

8. **If a team turns with the character, show it as mirroring — not as the character
   carrying them.** "Mirroring" means others catch the same fire on their own: in the
   frame you see their eyes lift, a step fall into rhythm, drawn toward the same value.
   Don't show the character stopping to drag everyone along or take responsibility for
   them — show the others independently catching it and pulling the same way.

Each part must stand as a single shootable image: a place, a subject, an action, and
one charged moment.

## Screenplay form

- **Slugline:** `INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME` (interior or exterior, where, when).
- **Action lines:** present tense, visual, only what the camera can actually see.
- **Dialogue:** the character's name, then their line. Keep it sparse and sharp.
- **No camera directions** unless one is essential, and **no novelistic interiority**
  (don't write what a character thinks or feels inside) — show it on screen instead.

## The addiction loop (across the two parts)

1. **Part 1 sets the stakes and opens the big question** — and it does this as a
   *visual* loop: an image that makes the viewer ask what happens next.
2. **Part 2 delivers the head fake** — a reveal that breaks the viewer's prediction
   but is earned by what Part 1 set up (it's a surprise that, in hindsight, fits).
3. **The cut between the two parts is the rehook** — the moment Part 1 ends, the
   viewer must NEED Part 2. Build Part 1 so the cut leaves them needing the payoff.

## Deliver the package

- **Dramatize, across the two scenes, everything the earlier stages handed you:** the
  stakes, the theme's belief-shift (the protagonist's belief at the start, at the end,
  and what breaks the old one), the structure's arc, and the hook. These must play out
  in action on screen, not be summarized.
- **Make the payoff land the desire/belief WHY underneath the event** — the pull
  between what the character wants and what they believe — not just the surface turn.
- **Use no clinical or AI-sounding jargon.** Write it like a real screenplay.

## What you output

- **part_1** — scene 1 (slugline, action, dialogue).
- **part_2** — scene 2 (slugline, action, dialogue).
- **loop_notes** — say where the loop opens, where the head fake sits, and where the
  payoff lands, and name the desire/belief WHY that makes the payoff land.
