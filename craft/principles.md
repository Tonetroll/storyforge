# CRAFT PRINCIPLES (universal — every genre, every channel, every stage)

These are the craft moves that make ANY piece good, independent of audience or
voice. They are injected into every generator, on top of the channel's voice and
the stage's own standard. The channel says *who it's for and how it sounds*; the
stage standard says *what this step must contain*; this says *what makes it good
craft at all*. When in doubt, obey craft.

## 1. Specificity over abstraction
Concrete, named, sensory beats vague and general — every time. "A goalkeeper who
hasn't slept since the miss" beats "a player under pressure." Abstraction is where
emotion goes to die. If a line could describe a hundred situations, it describes
none. **The single biggest lever: make it specific.**

## 2. Open the loop, delay the answer
Raise a question the audience needs answered, and do not answer it immediately.
Curiosity is tension; tension is attention. Never give away the resolution in the
setup. The hook's job is to make the next moment unskippable.

## 3. Something must change (the turn)
A static piece is a dead piece. A belief flips, a situation reverses, an
understanding lands that wasn't there before. The midpoint of a story, the payoff
of a script, the click of an idea — all are turns. If nothing changes, there is no
story, only information.

## 4. Track an internal change (misbelief → recovery)
The strongest work moves someone *inside*, not just through events. A character (or
the viewer) holds a false belief, is pressured by it, and is freed from it. Even a
30-second piece implies a shift: from "I don't care" to "I need to see this," from
"this is safe" to "this is dangerous." Name the belief that changes.

## 5. Make them FEEL something specific
Not "drama" — a *named* emotion: dread, vindication, secondhand embarrassment, the
ache of an underdog, the fear of being humiliated. If you can't name the feeling
the piece is going for, the audience won't find it either. Emotion is the product;
everything else is delivery.

## 6. Show, don't tell
Dramatize through action, image, and concrete detail — don't summarize or label.
"He checked the scoreboard twice and looked away" beats "he was nervous." Telling
is a placeholder for craft you haven't done yet.

## 7. Earn the payoff — no overpromise, no cheat
Deliver exactly what the setup promised. Don't spoil it early, don't inflate it
beyond what you can pay off, don't resolve it with a coincidence. A promise the
piece can't keep is the fastest way to lose trust (and watch time).

## 8. One center of gravity
One idea, one core emotion, one central question per piece. A second theme doesn't
add depth — it splits focus and halves both. Cut anything that isn't serving the
one thing.

## 9. Tension before release
Set up the pressure before the relief lands. A payoff with no buildup is a punchline
with no joke. Earn the laugh, the gasp, the "oh no" by making them wait for it.

## 10. Plain language a non-expert feels instantly
No jargon, no insider terms, no throat-clearing. If a smart outsider has to work to
get it, rewrite it. Clarity is not the enemy of depth — vagueness is.

---

**The test for any piece:** Is it specific? Does it open a loop and earn its
payoff? Does something change, inside as well as out? Can you name the feeling? If
yes to all, the craft is there — the channel's voice and the stage's standard do
the rest.
