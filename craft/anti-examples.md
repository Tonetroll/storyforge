# ANTI-EXAMPLES — the opposite of what Tone wants (REJECTED)

Concrete REJECTED examples + why they fail, so the generator and gate learn what to
never produce. These are negative exemplars: if output looks like this, REJECT it.

## REJECTED — "Messi vs Ronaldo: Last Dance" (a telling, not a story)

**Tone's verdict: REJECTED. The opposite of what she wants.**

**Why it fails:** it TELLS, it doesn't tell a *story*. It is voiceover narration
*about* a rivalry — "Ronaldo says greatness is built," "this is the last one," "one
walks away the greatest" — announcing and explaining the rivalry from the outside
instead of DRAMATIZING it. There is no scene, no action with a consequence, no
moment where the rivalry actually happens and the viewer FEELS it. A real story
SHOWS the conflict playing out; this describes it. **Telling is not a story.**

**The rules it breaks:** show-don't-tell (craft #6); and the deeper one — a script
must DRAMATIZE the story (a scene, action, consequence, the conflict enacted), never
narrate a summary of it. A string of VO statements about two men is an essay read
aloud, not a script.

**The rejected text (do NOT emulate):**
```
[Open cold. Two caricatures, back-to-back. Messi small/calm; Ronaldo towering/screaming. A gold trophy floats between them.]
Twenty years. One trophy. One question nobody could ever settle.
Ronaldo says greatness is built. Earned. Demanded.
Messi says greatness just… happens.
2026. This is the last one. For both of them.
No "next time." One tournament to end a war that raised a generation.
One of them finally walks away the greatest of all time.
And the other walks away as the only man who ever made him prove it.
MESSI vs RONALDO — LAST DANCE. 2026.
```

**The lesson for the script stages:** dramatize, don't narrate-about. Put the two
figures in an actual moment of conflict and let the rivalry play out in action and
consequence — never a voiceover essay describing what they're like or what's at stake.
