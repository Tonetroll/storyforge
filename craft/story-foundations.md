# STORY FOUNDATIONS — Tone's pre-writing framework (the spec)

Tone's foundational pre-writing questions. Every story must answer ALL of these
BEFORE it is written — for the antagonist as fully as the protagonist. The current
story doctrine answered the Protagonist and the Theme and DROPPED the Antagonist and
the Plot spine. This is the spec the story stage is being rebuilt around (Phase 1).
(There are more roles than this; for a two-figure conflict these are the core.)

## 1. The Protagonist (Who is this about?)
- Who is the main character? What does the reader need to know immediately? What is uncovered layer by layer?
- What do they consciously WANT? — their tangible, external goal (a job, solving a problem, falling in love).
- What do they internally NEED? — the underlying emotional flaw or lack (to learn forgiveness, trust, bravery).
- What is their worst FEAR? — what they are actively trying to avoid.

## 2. The Plot (What actually happens?)
- Why can't they just walk away? — the stakes must matter personally. What do they stand to lose if they fail?
- Who or what is the ANTAGONIST? — the primary opposing force standing between the protagonist and their goal.
- What is the "point of no return"? — the moment they enter a situation they cannot reverse without changing completely.
- How does it end? — even without exact details, the emotional or situational resolution.

## 3. The Theme (Why does this matter?)
- What is the core THEME? (love, betrayal, redemption, the corruptibility of power…)
- What does the character LEARN about this theme by the end? — the internal arc dictates how the message resonates.

---

**When the brief centers two figures:** the antagonist is not a "looming force" — he
is a second full character. Answer Section 1 (want / need / fear) for BOTH the
protagonist AND the antagonist, with their desires AND beliefs DIFFERENT, and the
engine of the story is the COLLISION of those differences — direct conflict, not two
parallel solo arcs. (It may feel like a rivalry, but need not be one.)

## BUILDING TENSION (the build-up beat) — escalate through ACTION into panic
Tone flagged the build-up as the WEAKEST part. It must NOT be a flat, narrated "the
pressure grew." It must ESCALATE through concrete ACTION: a surface problem that
loops and intensifies into deeper panic — an **escalating loop**. SHOW the character
physically trying to hold the facade together — furiously scrubbing off makeup,
aggressively brushing teeth, the small frantic actions of someone barely keeping it
in — before the facade finally cracks. Rising action you can see, not a line that
says tension is rising. (Belongs in the story stage's `build_up`, and the script's
escalation must carry it through.)
